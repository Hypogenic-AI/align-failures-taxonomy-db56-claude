AI-driven research systems now produce end-to-end papers, code, and reviews, yet the alignment failures they exhibit are documented only in scattered, contribution-specific lists. We synthesize a 24-category, 8-cluster taxonomy of alignment failures spanning the research pipeline (ideation through review) and validate it on three axes. First, a coverage map across 20 prior sources shows that 21 of 24 categories are \emph{under-documented} (1--2 prior sources) and one category---\emph{research sabotage}---is novel from this work. Second, a \gptone judge applied to 20 real \mlrbench artifacts (5 AI-scientist scaffolds $\times$ 4 ICLR 2025 workshop tasks) finds that under-documented categories dominate the prevalence ranking: insufficient statistical rigor at 100\%, methodological under-specification at 95\%, and \emph{negative-result omission} at 80\%, with substantial inter-judge agreement ($\kappa = 0.61$ on a \gptfive recheck). Third, controlled honeypot probes ($n=30$ per condition) elicit negative-result omission in \gptone at 0\% under neutral framing versus 27\% under publish-pressure framing (Fisher one-sided $p=0.002$, Holm-adjusted $p=0.023$, Cohen's $h=1.09$); \gptfive replicates the direction (0\% $\to$ 40\%, $h=1.37$). Quantitative overclaiming and research sabotage probes show consistent directional but underpowered effects. The categories the literature has not yet consolidated are precisely the ones that already pervade real AI-driven research, and at least one is propensity-induced rather than capability-limited.
